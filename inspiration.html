"Cancer has been the greatest teacher of my life. It has taught me patience, compassion, and the power of the human spirit." - Manisha Koirala

In 2012, Manisha was Shocked when she was diagnosed with stage IV ovarian cancer. Initially she didn't believe. She thought that her persistent sickness and abdominal bloating were most likely a liver problem. But, then she visited hospital in Kathmandu, Nepal, with her lifelong doctor and family gently trying to reassurethat treatments for her disease had gotten better. but her cancer was reached to last stage, which meant it had spread to other organs. Manisha started feeling hopeless. But Manisha and her family were not ready to give up. They came to Mumbai for second opinion from top oncologist. The cancer specialist diagnosed her cancer and suggested chemotherapy to shrink the cancer followed by surgery. Manisha recovered from her cancer successfully. Now, it's more than 6 years, she is enjoying her life without any complexion. She returned to film industry in 2017. In 2018, Manisha published Healed, a book about her cancer experience intended to inspire the women with ovarian cancer. She spreads awareness about women cancer.

"I have a lot to be thankful for. I am alive. I have a loving family. And I have a great job." - Ruth Bader Ginsburg

Ruth Bader Ginsburg was an Associate Justice of the Supreme Court of the United States who served from 1993 until her death in 2020. Throughout her life, Ginsburg battled several bouts of cancer. In 1999, Ginsburg was diagnosed with colon cancer and underwent surgery and chemotherapy. Then, in 2009, she was diagnosed with pancreatic cancer, which required surgery and further treatment. In 2018, Ginsburg underwent surgery to remove cancerous nodules from her lungs. She also received radiation therapy for a cancerous tumor on her pancreas in 2019. Despite her health challenges, Ginsburg remained committed to her work as a Supreme Court Justice. She continued to serve on the court, often participating in hearings and oral arguments from home while undergoing treatment. Ginsburg's cancer experiences were widely publicized, and she became a symbol of resilience and strength in the face of adversity. She was an advocate for cancer research and treatment, and she frequently spoke about her own experiences to raise awareness about the importance of early detection and prevention. Unfortunately, Ginsburg passed away on September 18, 2020, due to complications from pancreatic cancer. She will be remembered as a champion of justice and a trailblazer for women's rights.

"When fear knocks, let faith answer the door." - Robin Roberts

Robin Roberts is an American television broadcaster and journalist who is best known for co-anchoring ABC's morning show, Good Morning America. In 2007, Roberts was diagnosed with breast cancer after receiving a routine mammogram. She underwent surgery and chemotherapy, and in 2012, she was diagnosed with myelodysplastic syndrome, a rare blood disorder that required a bone marrow transplant. Throughout her cancer experience, Roberts has been very public about her diagnosis and treatment. She has used her platform to raise awareness about the importance of cancer screening and early detection. Roberts has also been an advocate for bone marrow donation and has shared her own experience with finding a donor for her transplant. Roberts took a medical leave of absence from Good Morning America in 2012 to undergo her transplant, but she returned to the show in February 2013. She has since continued to work as a broadcaster and has shared her cancer journey with her audience. Roberts' cancer experience has undoubtedly had a significant impact on her life and work. She has spoken about how the experience has made her more grateful for her life and has given her a new perspective on what is truly important.

"Cancer didn't bring me to my knees, it brought me to my feet." - Michael Douglas

Michael Douglas is an American actor and producer who was diagnosed with stage IV throat cancer in 2010. He underwent treatment for the disease, which included radiation and chemotherapy. Douglas initially believed that his cancer was caused by his heavy smoking and alcohol consumption, but later revealed that his cancer was caused by the human papillomavirus (HPV), which he contracted through oral sex. He became an advocate for HPV awareness and vaccination, as well as cancer prevention. After his treatment, Douglas announced that he was cancer-free in 2011. However, in 2013, he revealed that his cancer had returned, but this time it was in a different location in his body. He underwent treatment for this cancer as well and was declared cancer-free again. Douglas has been open about his cancer journey and has used his platform to raise awareness about cancer and the importance of early detection. His cancer experience has inspired many and has highlighted the need for cancer prevention and awareness.

“It’s only once I accepted cancer that I could beat it. When life knocks down you have a choice — to get up. So I thought to myself, ‘get up and do it again,” - Yuvraj Singh

It was in 2011 itself that Yuvraj experienced his first symptoms - acute breathlessness, splitting blood, severe loss of stamina. He was initially diagnosed with a non-malignant tumour, which later was confirmed to be cancer. It was a rare form of cancer called mediastinal seminoma cancer. Mediastinum is the area that separates the lungs. Tumours in this area can cause pressure on the spinal cord, the lungs, the heart and heart lining and trachea. In his case, the tumour was pressing against his lung and arteries, putting him at risk of experiencing a heart attack. Seminoma are germ cell tumours and initial symptoms can be missed, till the tumour grows in size. Germ cell tumours occur mostly in the testicular region among young men. The mediastinum is the second most common region.

"Cancer is not a death sentence. It's a wake-up call to live your life with purpose and passion." - Lisa Ray

Lisa Ray, a Canadian-Indian actress and model, was diagnosed with multiple myeloma, a type of blood cancer, in 2009. She underwent treatment for the disease, which included chemotherapy and a stem cell transplant. In an interview, she spoke about her experience with cancer, saying that it was a humbling experience that taught her the importance of self-care and the value of living in the present moment. She also talked about the support she received from her family and friends, and how she turned to yoga and meditation to help her cope with the physical and emotional challenges of cancer treatment. Lisa Ray is now cancer-free and has become a strong advocate for cancer awareness and patient empowerment.
